NEON Water Chemistry

See what is dissolved in a stream or a lake, and how it changes through the year. NEON is a network of research sites across the United States, paid for by the National Science Foundation, that measures water, plants, animals, and weather the same way everywhere, year after year. This free app lets you explore the chemistry of the water at those sites. Pick a spot, pick something like nitrogen, salt, or acidity, and watch it rise and fall with the seasons. Then compare sites across the country. No account, just pick a site and start.

What you can do here

Pick one thing in the water. Watch it through the year, then across sites.

It starts with a real water sample at a field site, either scooped by hand or read by a sensor in the stream. The app lines all of those up so you can pick one thing that is dissolved in the water, see how it changes over time, and compare one site to another. The numbers behind every chart are always shown.

How it stays honest
Everything here is real NEON data, saved ahead of time so the app loads right away with nothing to wait for. Every number tells you how many measurements it is based on, so you can see when a result rests on plenty of data and when it rests on only a little. When the app draws a line between two things, it shows how well the line fits and warns when readings taken close together in time can make a link look stronger than it is. When it checks whether two things move together, it uses a method that holds up even when the numbers are lopsided, treats anything based on fewer than 8 measurements as shaky, and reminds you that checking many things at once turns up some matches by luck. The seasonal pattern is pulled straight from the real record, not a smooth curve made up to look nice. Amounts too low for the instruments to measure are marked, not hidden. Each thing in the water is named and labeled correctly (bromide is not bicarbonate, chloride is not chlorine). This is a free tool for learning and exploring.
